The 90 year storm

My stay in Sydney has been following the plot of Point Break with unnerving accuracy. So much so that I'm seriously considering applying to the FBI. And changing my name to Johnny Putney.

In the movie, it starts with this gorgeous hunk arriving in a new town. Then, for reasons unknown he tries to learn how to surf. He's awful at it. Then he befriends a local hotty to teach him to surf. (If you’ve been following the blog, then so far the movie and real life are in lock step - it’s uncanny, right?). Then he breaks a rib learning to surf (OK so a bit of deviation here - Keanu Reeves was only acting. No danger for him. I was doing it for real, dude). Then there’s a beaudacious robbery. Then it starts raining and doesn’t stop. The 90 year storm has arrived. Time to test my surfing skills to the max...

Re-reading the above paragraph leads me to the only obvious conclusion. I am the living breathing embodiment of Keanu Reeves, made flesh (the one you see in the movies is clearly made of wood - you can tell by the acting). Helen can play the local hotty (brownie points, please). Though she hasn’t spent much time teaching me to surf. Or even being interested in my new found passion. Or indeed giving me much sympathy over the phantom broken rib (it really hurts).

But the 90 year storm was real enough. Oh boy was it ever. This was the real deal. For once the news was not exaggerated. The wind and rain were unrelentingly strong. Trees were blown over. Cars were blown over. Waves over 14 metres high were recorded just off the coast. Whole beaches were upped and moved 25 metres closer to the land. The harbour is a brown sludge of topsoil and sewage (nice for those surfers). It felt like it would never end.

Our rental house, like most houses in Sydney I’m guessing, started leaking water. We needed towels to mop it all up. We didn’t have enough towels. The garden furniture was being blown across the garden (these are not small lightweight chairs either). Water was being blown in under the front door. Outside, cars were being moved by the wind. People trying to cross flooded roads were swept away. Some, unfortunately, lost their lives.

Afterwards, the sun reappeared as if nothing had happened and things started to dry out. But it will take a while. Rivers had burst their banks. Some northern beaches will never be the same. On Bondi, the beach had been blown back towards the land. The car parks were full of sand. The skate park was closed (bugger!) and 5 large diggers started the slow process of pushing the sand back towards the sea. Most beaches were closed. Swimming was banned.

So I grabbed my board. Stuck it under my arm. And headed out into the waves. A storm like this only comes around once a lifetime. No way I was going to miss out.

Even if I hadn’t quite learned to surf.
