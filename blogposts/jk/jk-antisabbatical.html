The antisabbatical

Suddenly I'm six years old again. I can't understand anyone. I don't know how anything works. I don't know how to cross the road. Even going to the toilet is a challenge. I feel vulnerable.

And what a joy it is to feel like that. Out of place. Out of control. Uncertain. Lost. Welcome to the world. Travelling puts you in this position time and again. If I feel six, how does my six year old feel? On your own, you can revel in this feeling. It's part of the fun. But I'm in charge. I'm in control. We are a family.

But I cede control. I want to be lost. I like being lost. I'm on an antisabbatical. The first of many I hope. Rather than working for 10 years to get a couple of months off for good behaviour, the sabbatical justifying the work, why not work for a bit and earn enough to take off travelling for 2 years or more? The antisabbatical being the goal, not the ten years good behaviour.

It gives work a real purpose. You have real drive to work hard. The pain of the work a psychic importance. It allows you time to plan the next trip. It gives you time to look forward to the trip. Builds it up into something even bigger than it is. And it makes you feel like you've earned the trip. You deserve the trip. That's important.

The hardest decision is the decision to leave. Like all big decisions it feels impossible. People don't make it easy. They hold you back. Keep you rooted. They don't want you to go. My advice is not to tell anyone. Debate and argue and rationalise with yourself or your family but no one else. And then once made, that's it. It's done. Everything else falls into place. The planning and research and booking is fun. And easy.

And the next big decision feels easier.
