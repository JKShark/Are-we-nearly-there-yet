A different world

We landed at a brand new airport, 5 days old. We drove along a brand new highway, 3 days old. And then we landed on a different planet.

The contrast between Dubai and Hanoi is so stark, we might as well have landed on Mars.

The obvious differences in wealth, weather, skyline, noise and traffic were hard on us all. I wanted to write about it immediately but I knew that the shock wouldn't make me coherent.

We've been in Hanoi for two days and I'm now getting used to it. As with all my posts, when I talk about Hanoi, a city of 7 million, I really mean the 3 square kilometres around our hotel which I've seen. The old town. Centred on Lake Kiem. An area well known to any backpacker.

It is crowded and dirty and full of traffic and so different as to be utterly beguiling.

There are scooters everywhere. On the pavements (sometimes parked, sometimes heading towards you), all over the roads (both sides), on one way streets (both ways) and going through red lights with abandon. If you wait at a zebra crossing, you could well die there before anyone stops for you.

There is a knack to walking around here. First rule is to walk slowly. Very slowly. So the traffic sees you and has time to avoid you. Don't bother trying to walk on the pavement. Walk on the right hand side of the road (they (sort of) drive on the right). And hold on to your children very tightly. To cross the road, walk out into the traffic slowly and keep going, hoping that they see you and go round you. It feels completely mad. But it works. We haven't died yet. And we haven't seen any accidents which defies belief.

I love the fact that they have entire streets here dedicated to a single product. There is a silk road, a medicine road, a locksmith road, a baby products road, a metal bashing road, a scooter fixing road etc (my translations may be a bit off but you get the drift).

But the 'shops' are also a complete mix. Initially, they all look like someone's front room stuffed with junk. Many of them are in fact just that. They used to have to pay tax based on how wide the house was. So all shops are very narrow and very deep. That means it's very hard to tell what a shop is like from the front. You can have a junk shop right next to a new Adidas shop, next to a knock-off handbag shop, next to an expensive silk shop.

Turns out that not everywhere in the world is like Putney. Who knew?
