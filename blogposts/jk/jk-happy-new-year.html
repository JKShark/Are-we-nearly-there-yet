Happy New Year (part 2)

No smog. No traffic. Polite locals. Good food. I feel like I've visited the wrong city. Where is the real Beijing?

New Year is a big deal here. Bigger than back home. Everyone gets a week off for a start, which helps New Years Eve go off with a bang. Literally.

90% of the world's fireworks are sold in China (one of many stats I've been told with no possible way of verifying it. You'll have to take my word for it). On New Year's Eve, from about 6pm onwards, the noise of fireworks and firecrackers makes us jump, even in the safety of our hotel room. Flashes of light catch our eyes as we wander the streets, reflected in the glass skyscrapers all around us so it feels as if we’re at the centre of a kaleidoscopic storm.

Despite this, we can't find a single person actually setting off any fireworks. Disappointing. We were told that we'd see fireworks being set off on the streets. We'd even acquired our own firework (from Panda Fireworks, natch), an enormous red tube of death with instructions only in symbolic calligraphy. The kids, though, were excited to see Daddy dice with death by setting it off.

But we see no one. We wander the freezing streets carrying our conical explosive, walking past policemen every couple of hundred yards. They are an effective deterrent to the locals - and we don't even know the rules here. If there's one place I don't feel like breaking the rules in front of the local armed police, then it's here in China.

Eventually we give up and head back to the roof top bar at the hotel. It's an outdoor bar and it's just started snowing. The gas heaters they've lit are providing far too little heat to thaw five cold and increasingly grumpy westerners. A necked cocktail and a quick assessment and appreciation of the fantastic views and we head back inside to huddle together to warm up.

Helen and I do see in the New Year, accompanied by the local equivalent of Jools Holland and what could be an X factor style talent competition (it's very difficult to tell what is going on). 700 million people are watching with us, making it the most watched TV show in the world (another of those unreliable stats).

We fall asleep to the rat-a-tat of firecrackers still being set off. They start again at 6am. Drawing the curtains back on the year of the goat, the blue sky has been replaced by gunpowder smog.

It's good to see you, Beijing.
