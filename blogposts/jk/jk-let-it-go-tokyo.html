Let it go, Tokyo

The way leads ever on and it's to China that we head today. Another new country for us, another new experience, another step into an unknown world. But what a blast it's been in Japan. As finales go, a day watching thousands of locals going ga-ga over Mickey Mouse and friends is hard to beat. Hearing Donald Duck announce in Japanese that we have arrived at our floor (in the lift) runs it a close second.

It was dark and bitterly cold by the time we left Disneyland, with the sound of "Let it go" being sung in Japanese all around us. They really love the Mouse here. We thought the cold and the time of year might mean we'd have the park to ourselves. Well, that was naïve. When the park opened, we were already inside but a minute later there were thousands (yes, thousands) of people running (yes, running) to join queues. It was so busy the queues for popcorn were half an hour long.

An hour into the day and the kids were both crying. It was an inauspicious start. The problem was me. I'm a big kid. I'd been on Space Mountain before, twenty years ago and I wanted to do it again. I knew the queues could be bad so we headed straight there. Three minutes of being flung around in the dark and I was grinning but the kids were in tears.

They loved everything else though. And we did too. Yes, it was American but done in such a Japanese way that it had a twist that made full on Americanness different somehow, almost endearing.

The whole of Japan has been just so different. So different to anywhere we've been. A modern, advanced nation with a unique approach. It just shows you, there is always more than one way to get to an answer. Whatever answer you're looking for.

We've seen neon, vast cities, beautiful mountains, amazing public transport, onsens, strange inedible food, taxi drivers with gloves (who never know where they are going), faceless facemasks, politeness everywhere (such a delight), lots of bowing (I am going to struggle to say thank you without bowing now), lots of people employed to do jobs that aren't jobs (three people with red glowing batons to point out the roadworks up ahead which themselves have signs and cones laid out), useless cash machines that no foreigner can use, vending machines everwhere (we must get these introduced back home), heated toilet seats (we are so going to get a Japanese toilet installed when we get back), deep fried breaded pork sandwiches (clean and lean surely?), breakfast buffets that defy time and are surely meant for lunch or dinner (noodles, miso soup, raw fish, grilled sardines, salad, deep fried chicken, cold scrambled egg, warm scrambled egg, rice, pickles), and tofu.

So much tofu. There are restaurants that just serve tofu. There is creamed tofu, boiled tofu, fried tofu, tofu with noodles and tofu soup. Whatever they did to it, it still tasted awful. Never dive into a bowl of yoghurt again without checking first, in case it is tofu. A big mistake, not to be repeated. Like thinking a bowl full of pickled onions is actually a bowl full of lychees.

So thank you, Japan, for a wonderful few weeks. You’ve been a difficult friend to love but impossible not to. Next time, I’d like to know a bit more local language to make things easier. But until the next time, ‘a rigatoni’.
