The right to breathe

The Internet is dead. It's a familiar refrain. And now I can't breathe.

It's our lifeline. Our drug of choice. It connects us except when it doesn't. We've had an internet connection most places we've been, which is remarkable, given where we've been. Vietnam has probably been the best. Free wifi everywhere. Cables everywhere. You can literally see your messages zing along the wires above your head.

The speed has been variable though. It's never been fast. Dial up speeds for the most part. Remember them? Select a website, click and then go and do something else while it loads up.

You may wonder why we're bothered. We don't have emails to check or urgent news to catch up on (in fact I am trying to avoid all news, which is another story). But the internet remains key to this whole trip. Planning our days out, booking our flights and accommodation, finding and downloading books to read, teaching the kids (apps and online teaching tools), downloading movies (yeah, fat chance), researching the strange customs we come across. They all need t'internet.

And I do like my sport. Playing it. Watching it. But I've not seen any of the cricket World Cup, none of the six nations rugby and not a single match of Man United's glorious rise to the top of the Premiership (I assume they're top. They usually are by now).

It's a bit like going back in time visiting these small Asian isles. No TV. No newspapers. Just splendid isolation. We even have an outdoor bathroom here. Something common in England not too long ago but not a thing from my generation. It doesn't feel like a backward step here. Waking up and showering under bright blue skies everyday is something I'll miss. It feels like a luxury. Though sharing the loo with the mosquitoes, snails, frogs, millipedes and lizards is something I won't...

I wonder what this island was like before air conditioning. The heat here is something else. Mid 30s everyday of the year (more or less) and humid as hell, at least at the moment. The humidity makes it feel even hotter. We went to visit Ubud yesterday. The centre of Bali its artistic cultural heart. We had lunch with Murni at her institution of a restaurant on the side of a river valley (utterly gorgeous) and then wandered around the town. But boy was it hot. Our pace reduced to a crawl. We were all sweating. You spend your time here always being slightly damp. Whether from the humidity itself or the perspiration dripping down your back. Our shop selection ended up being based on the temperature of the shop. Old cute and open fronted was out. New, doors, air conditioning was most definitely in.

We’re at the end of the wet season and we’ve seen some torrential rain (although only once to be fair, when it filled the road and submerged the pavements. The rest of the time it has deigned to rain in the dark.). We've been told it’s less humid in June and July and we'll have to take their word for it. But right now, it's hard to imagine anything getting done at this time of year. And before air conditioning this place was surely uninhabitable?

But I'm safely ensconced in the shade right now. Only my thumbs moving. The sweat temporarily being held at bay.

And I can breathe again.
