Water divining

We were searching for water. Helen holding on tight to the divining stick. In this wet land, standing on a beach, we were looking for more. And we wanted it to burn.

I’d chosen our base, on some distant promontory, to be close to the Beach. It looked close. But using a globe to plot our course was an error. On a globe, 50km is no distance at all. When it came to it, winding roads and mountains kept us apart. But I was determined to go. I knew the kids would love it.

I have this strange sensation whenever we head towards a ‘proper' tourist attraction. Somewhere that people are supposed to visit. I have this feeling that we’re doing something wrong. That everyone else knows something that we don’t. I’m not sure why. I start thinking about whether it’s going to be open. Should I have checked? Should I have called ahead? We’re going to places out of season. Will people think we’re mad? I’m not sure why I care. Maybe it’s the only thing I have left to worry about. Or maybe because it’s my idea to go to these places, I want them to be good.

Anyway, we make it to the Beach. To look for water. Why wouldn’t people think we’re mad?

Thankfully we’re not alone. There are other mad people out here too. Carrying spades. We thought we could use our hands but there are warnings not to. So I ask in the only shop whether they have a spade I can borrow. An odd question in most places. Here though, they have loads.

With an hour of light left, we head to the beach. The Hot Water Beach. There is a river under the sand which comes from deep within the earth and spills out onto the beach at about 60°C. Enough to burn your hands or your feet. We know where to go (roughly) and so do others. But inevitably, no one really knows what they’re doing. There are no locals here. We hear snatches of English, French even Scottish but no Kiwi. So people start randomly digging holes in the sand. If you start to dig a hole, then others come and join you, assuming you know what you’re doing.

We don’t.

But it’s very friendly. Strangers coming together to try to find the source. Coming up with theories and strategies. Low tide is coming but it’s not here yet. There are some futile attempts to hold back the sea. The sun is setting and it’s getting colder. Lots of people are standing in holes on the beach trying to persuade themselves that the water at their feet is not cold. You seek a second opinion. But the answer’s no. It’s still cold.

Suddenly, there’s some excitement near the shore. People think they’ve found the spot and a crowd gathers to start digging. We’re too cool to get sucked into the crowd. We continue to plough our own furrow (literally). The kids are, at least, having a ball. It’s like a weird party on the beach with everyone (even the adults) digging holes and getting soaked and filthy. What’s not to love?

Eventually the sun sets and we don’t fancy negotiating the beach in the dark. Reluctantly, before we leave, we join the crowd and dip our feet in their pool. It’s maybe a bit warm. But it’s definitely not hot. It’s nowhere close to burning. It’s about as hot as you’d expect seawater to be having had ten people stand in it for half an hour. They’re convinced they’ve found the source. I’m not so sure...

I don’t know whether the river has cooled down. Or whether we weren’t in the right place. Or the tide was too high that day. A lot of the photos online do seem a bit '70s. Maybe I’m just being paranoid. Worried that we’ve been ‘had’. That it’s all an elaborate con.

I don’t care. Whatever we were doing, it was a lot of fun. The kids all pass out exhausted on the ride home. I have blisters and an aching back. A bit of manual labour for these soft hands. No bad thing.

We’ll just have to keep on searching.
